## üéØ What is Product Strategy

### **Definition**

**Product Strategy** is the plan for how your product will achieve its vision and win in the market.

**Strategy Answers:**
- **What** are we building? (and NOT building)
- **Who** are we building for? (and NOT for)
- **Why** will we win? (competitive advantage)
- **How** will we achieve our vision? (approach)

---

### **Why Strategy Matters**

**Without Strategy:**
- ‚ùå Roadmap becomes feature list
- ‚ùå Team lacks direction
- ‚ùå No competitive differentiation
- ‚ùå Reactive to market
- ‚ùå Resource waste

**With Strategy:**
- ‚úÖ Clear direction
- ‚úÖ Aligned team
- ‚úÖ Competitive moat
- ‚úÖ Proactive choices
- ‚úÖ Efficient execution

---

### **Good Strategy vs. Bad Strategy**

**Bad Strategy:**
- Vague mission statements
- List of goals without how
- Copying competitors
- "We'll be the best at everything"
- Lots of buzzwords, no substance

**Good Strategy:**
- Specific choices
- Clear trade-offs
- Unique positioning
- Defensible advantage
- Concrete approach

**Example - Bad Strategy:**
"We will leverage AI to revolutionize productivity and become the leading platform for knowledge workers globally."

**Example - Good Strategy:**
"We will build the first AI writing assistant specifically for B2B SaaS marketers, focusing on SEO blog posts. We'll differentiate by integrating with marketing analytics tools and optimizing for conversion, not just content quality. We'll win by building the best data flywheel: better content ‚Üí better SEO data ‚Üí better AI."

## üèóÔ∏è Vision, Strategy, Roadmap, Features Hierarchy

### **The Product Hierarchy**

```
Vision
  ‚Üì
Strategy
  ‚Üì
Roadmap
  ‚Üì

```

**Vision** = Where we're going (aspirational)
**Strategy** = How we'll get there (plan)
**Roadmap** = What we'll build when (execution)
**Features** = Specific capabilities (tactical)

---

### **Vision**

**What it is:** The aspirational future state

**Characteristics:**
- Inspirational
- Long-term (5-10 years)
- Unchanging (mostly)
- Simple to understand

**Examples:**

**GitHub:** "Every developer has a copilot"

**Notion:** "Make software toolmaking ubiquitous"

**ChatGPT:** "Build safe AGI that benefits humanity"

**Your AI Product:** "Enable every writer to publish 10x more high-quality content"

---

### **Strategy**

**What it is:** The plan to achieve the vision

**Characteristics:**
- Specific choices
- Medium-term (1-3 years)
- Adaptable based on learning
- Differentiating

**Components:**
- Target market
- Value proposition
- Competitive advantage
- Key capabilities
- Not doing (trade-offs)

**Example - GitHub Copilot Strategy:**

**Target:** Professional developers (not beginners)
**Value Prop:** 10x faster coding via AI autocomplete
**Advantage:** Exclusive access to GitHub's code data
**Capabilities:** LLM integration, IDE plugins, code understanding
**Not doing:** Visual programming, teaching coding, full automation

---

### **Roadmap**

**What it is:** The sequenced plan of what to build

**Characteristics:**
- Time-bound (6-18 months)
- Theme-based
- Measurable outcomes
- Flexible

**Example - AI Writing Tool Roadmap:**

**Q1:** Foundation (core AI writing, basic editor)
**Q2:** Differentiation (SEO optimization, analytics integration)
**Q3:** Moat (data flywheel, personalization)
**Q4:** Expansion (new content types, team features)

---

### **Features**

**What it is:** Specific capabilities users can use

**Characteristics:**
- Concrete
- User-facing
- Testable
- Shippable

**Example:**
- "AI autocomplete for blog intros"
- "SEO keyword suggestions"
- "Tone adjustment controls"

---

### **Connecting the Layers**

**Bad Product Management:**
Vision ‚Üí Features (skip strategy & roadmap)

**Good Product Management:**
Vision ‚Üí Strategy ‚Üí Roadmap ‚Üí Features

**Every feature should answer:**
- ‚úÖ How does this connect to strategy?
- ‚úÖ Why this feature and not others?
- ‚úÖ How does this differentiate us?

## üìä Core Strategy Frameworks

### **Framework 1: Playing to Win (P&G Strategy)**

**Developed by A.G. Lafley (P&G CEO) and Roger Martin**

**Five Strategy Choices:**

**1. What is our Winning Aspiration?**
- What do we want to achieve?
- What does "winning" mean?

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
"Be the default AI pair programmer for every professional developer"

**2. Where will we Play?**
- Which customers?
- Which geographies?
- Which products?
- Which channels?

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
- **Customers:** Professional developers (not students/beginners)
- **Geographies:** Global (English first, then expand)
- **Products:** Code IDEs (VS Code, JetBrains)
- **Channels:** GitHub, developer tools ecosystem

**3. How will we Win?**
- What's our competitive advantage?
- Why will customers choose us?

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
- Trained on billions of lines of GitHub code
- Deep IDE integration
- GitHub trust & brand
- First-mover advantage in AI coding

**4. What Capabilities must we have?**
- What must we be great at?
- What will it take to win?

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
- LLM expertise (partnership with OpenAI)
- IDE integration excellence
- Code understanding
- Developer experience design
- Data pipeline (GitHub ‚Üí training)

**5. What Management Systems are required?**
- How will we support these capabilities?
- What org structure, processes, metrics?

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
- AI/ML team integrated with product
- Fast iteration cycles
- Usage metrics dashboard
- Developer feedback loops

### **Framework 2: DHM Model (Gibson Biddle)**

**Developed by Gibson Biddle (Netflix VP Product)**

**DHM = Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways**

**Three Questions:**

**1. How will we DELIGHT customers?**
- What creates joy/value?
- What makes product beloved?

**2. In ways that are HARD TO COPY?**
- What's our defensible advantage?
- What can't competitors replicate easily?

**3. In MARGIN-ENHANCING ways?**
- How does this make business better?
- Revenue increase or cost decrease?

---

#### **DHM Examples**

**Netflix (Gibson's example):**
- **Delight:** Personalized recommendations, binge-worthy content
- **Hard to Copy:** Recommendation algorithm (years of viewing data), Original content
- **Margin-Enhancing:** Better recommendations ‚Üí higher retention ‚Üí lower churn cost

**GitHub Copilot:**
- **Delight:** Write code 10x faster, feels like magic
- **Hard to Copy:** Exclusive access to GitHub's code dataset, IDE integration depth
- **Margin-Enhancing:** High willingness to pay ($10-20/month), low incremental cost

**Your AI Writing Tool:**
- **Delight:** [How will you delight users?]
- **Hard to Copy:** [What's unique to you?]
- **Margin-Enhancing:** [How does this improve business?]

### **Framework 3: Blue Ocean Strategy**

**Create uncontested market space instead of competing in red oceans.**

**Red Ocean (Competitive Market):**
- Fight for existing demand
- Beat the competition
- Exploit existing demand
- Make value-cost tradeoff

**Blue Ocean (New Market Space):**
- Create new demand
- Make competition irrelevant
- Create and capture new demand
- Break value-cost tradeoff

---

#### **Four Actions Framework**

**To create Blue Ocean, ask:**

**1. ELIMINATE:**
What factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?

**2. REDUCE:**
What factors should be reduced well below industry standard?

**3. RAISE:**
What factors should be raised well above industry standard?

**4. CREATE:**
What factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

---

#### **Blue Ocean Example: ChatGPT**

**ELIMINATE:**
- ‚ùå Complex setup/configuration
- ‚ùå Domain-specific training required
- ‚ùå Technical expertise needed

**REDUCE:**
- ‚¨áÔ∏è Cost (free tier vs. expensive enterprise AI)
- ‚¨áÔ∏è Learning curve
- ‚¨áÔ∏è Time to value

**RAISE:**
- ‚¨ÜÔ∏è Natural language interface quality
- ‚¨ÜÔ∏è Versatility (one tool for many tasks)
- ‚¨ÜÔ∏è Accessibility

**CREATE:**
- ‚ú® Conversational AI for everyone
- ‚ú® General-purpose AI assistant
- ‚ú® Viral sharing of conversations

**Result:** Created new market of "AI assistants for consumers," not competing with enterprise AI tools.

---

### **Framework 4: Jobs to be Done (JTBD)**

**Products are "hired" to do jobs. Strategy should focus on the job, not the product.**

**Job Statement Format:**
"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

**Example - GitHub Copilot:**
"When I'm writing boilerplate code, I want to autocomplete entire functions, so I can focus on creative problem-solving instead of syntax."

**Strategy Implication:**
- Compete with all solutions to that job (not just similar products)
- Understand context (situation) deeply
- Optimize for outcome, not features

## ‚öîÔ∏è Competitive Strategy

### **Porter's Five Forces**

**Classic framework for understanding competitive dynamics**

**1. Threat of New Entrants**
- How easy for new competitors to enter?
- Barriers to entry?

**AI Market Example:**
- **Low barriers:** LLMs are commoditized, APIs available
- **High barriers:** Proprietary data, distribution, brand

**Strategy:** Build barriers (data moats, network effects, brand)

---

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
- How much power do suppliers have?
- Can they raise prices?

**AI Market Example:**
- **OpenAI, Google, Anthropic** have high power (few LLM providers)
- Risk: They could raise API prices or compete with you

**Strategy:** Multi-model support, consider open-source, build switching costs

---

**3. Bargaining Power of Buyers**
- How much power do customers have?
- Can they negotiate?

**AI Market Example:**
- **Consumer:** Low power (small individual buyers)
- **Enterprise:** High power (big contracts, can negotiate)

**Strategy:** Focus on consumers or SMBs for pricing power

---

**4. Threat of Substitutes**
- What alternatives exist?
- How easily can users switch?

**AI Market Example:**
- ChatGPT can substitute for many AI tools
- Human labor can substitute for AI

**Strategy:** Differentiate deeply, make switching costly, provide unique value

---

**5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors**
- How intense is competition?
- Price wars? Feature races?

**AI Market Example:**
- **Intense:** Everyone adding AI features
- **Race:** To best model, fastest inference, lowest cost

**Strategy:** Find niche, build moats, focus on specific use case

## ü§ñ AI Product Strategy Patterns

### **Pattern 1: Horizontal AI Platform**

**Strategy:** Build general-purpose AI tool for broad use cases

**Examples:**
- ChatGPT (general AI assistant)
- Midjourney (general image generation)
- Runway (general video editing)

**Advantages:**
- Large TAM
- Broad appeal
- Platform potential

**Challenges:**
- Intense competition
- Hard to differentiate
- Commoditization risk

**When to Choose:**
- You have unique data
- You have distribution advantage
- You can be first/best

---

### **Pattern 2: Vertical AI Solution**

**Strategy:** Build AI for specific industry/use case

**Examples:**
- Harvey (AI for lawyers)
- Glass Health (AI for doctors)
- Jasper (AI for marketers)

**Advantages:**
- Deeper differentiation
- Higher willingness to pay
- Domain moats

**Challenges:**
- Smaller TAM
- Need domain expertise
- Slower scaling

**When to Choose:**
- You have domain expertise
- Industry has unique needs
- Willing to go deep

---

### **Pattern 3: AI Feature/Copilot**

**Strategy:** Add AI to existing product category

**Examples:**
- GitHub Copilot (AI in code editor)
- Notion AI (AI in docs)
- Grammarly (AI in writing)

**Advantages:**
- Leverage existing product
- Clear use case
- Existing users

**Challenges:**
- May not be core differentiator
- Risk of commoditization
- Dependent on base product

**When to Choose:**
- You have existing product
- AI enhances core value
- Natural fit

---

### **Pattern 4: AI Infrastructure/Platform**

**Strategy:** Build tools for other AI builders

**Examples:**
- LangChain (AI app framework)
- Pinecone (vector database)
- Weights & Biases (ML ops)

**Advantages:**
- B2B pricing power
- Technical moat
- Network effects

**Challenges:**
- Technical complexity
- Longer sales cycles
- Developer-focused

**When to Choose:**
- You have deep AI expertise
- You solve infra problem
- Developer audience

---

### **Pattern 5: AI Data Flywheel**

**Strategy:** Build product where AI gets better with usage

**Examples:**
- Grammarly (more users ‚Üí more corrections ‚Üí better model)
- Duolingo (more learners ‚Üí better adaptive lessons)
- Tesla (more miles ‚Üí better autopilot)

**Advantages:**
- Compounding advantage
- Defensible moat
- Winner-take-most dynamics

**Challenges:**
- Cold start problem
- Slow initial progress
- Privacy concerns

**When to Choose:**
- You can collect valuable data
- Users benefit from network
- Feedback loop is clear

## üè∞ Building Defensible Moats

### **AI Product Moats**

**Traditional software moats don't always work for AI. Here's what does:**

---

### **Moat 1: Proprietary Data**

**What it is:** Unique data that improves your AI

**Examples:**
- **GitHub Copilot:** Billions of lines of code from GitHub
- **Google Search:** Decades of search behavior
- **Grammarly:** Millions of writing corrections

**How to Build:**
- Collect data from product usage
- Partner for exclusive data
- Create incentive to share data

**Defensibility:** High (data is hard to replicate)

---

### **Moat 2: Network Effects**

**What it is:** Product gets better as more people use it

**Types:**

**Direct Network Effects:**
More users ‚Üí more value per user

**Example:** LinkedIn (more professionals ‚Üí better network)

**Data Network Effects:**
More users ‚Üí more data ‚Üí better AI ‚Üí attracts more users

**Example:** Waze (more drivers ‚Üí better traffic data ‚Üí better routes)

**How to Build:**
- Design for virality
- Make sharing valuable
- Build marketplace dynamics

**Defensibility:** Very High (winner-take-most)

---

### **Moat 3: Brand & Trust**

**What it is:** Users trust your AI over competitors

**Examples:**
- **OpenAI:** Synonymous with safe, capable AI
- **Google:** Trusted search and information
- **Grammarly:** Trusted writing accuracy

**How to Build:**
- Consistent quality
- Transparency about AI
- Safety/ethics leadership
- Thought leadership

**Defensibility:** Medium (can be eroded, but sticky)

---

### **Moat 4: Integration Depth**

**What it is:** Deep integration into workflows/tools

**Examples:**
- **GitHub Copilot:** Native IDE integration
- **Grammarly:** Browser extension everywhere
- **Notion AI:** Embedded in workspace

**How to Build:**
- Focus on one platform first
- Make integration seamless
- High switching cost

**Defensibility:** Medium (can be replicated, but effort)

---

### **Moat 5: Specialized Models**

**What it is:** Fine-tuned models for specific use case

**Examples:**
- **Codex** (specialized for code)
- **Med-PaLM** (specialized for medical)
- **Bloomberg GPT** (specialized for finance)

**How to Build:**
- Domain-specific training data
- Custom fine-tuning
- Specialized evaluation

**Defensibility:** Medium (models can be copied, but data is harder)

---

### **Moat 6: Speed & Cost Advantage**

**What it is:** Faster inference or lower cost than competitors

**Examples:**
- **Groq:** 10x faster inference
- **Together AI:** Cheaper open-source models

**How to Build:**
- Infrastructure optimization
- Model optimization
- Economies of scale

**Defensibility:** Low (temporary, tech advantage erodes)

---

### **Moat 7: Regulatory/Compliance**

**What it is:** Meet compliance requirements competitors can't

**Examples:**
- **HIPAA-compliant AI** for healthcare
- **SOC2/GDPR compliance** for enterprise
- **Industry certifications**

**How to Build:**
- Early investment in compliance
- Legal/security expertise
- Audits and certifications

**Defensibility:** Medium (barrier to entry, but replicable)

---

### **Moat Combination Strategy**

**The best AI products combine multiple moats:**

**Example: Grammarly**
- ‚úÖ Proprietary Data (billions of corrections)
- ‚úÖ Network Effects (more users ‚Üí better AI)
- ‚úÖ Brand & Trust (known for accuracy)
- ‚úÖ Integration Depth (everywhere users write)
- ‚úÖ Specialized Models (writing-specific)

**Result:** Very defensible, hard to displace

---

### **Example: AI Email Assistant Strategy**

**Where We Are:**
Email is overwhelming. Knowledge workers spend 3+ hours/day in email, much of it low-value (reading, triaging, responding to routine messages).

**Where We're Going:**
Every professional has an AI email assistant that handles 80% of email automatically, freeing them for high-value work.

**Why We'll Win:**
- **Data Moat:** Exclusive access to 10M+ business email threads (partnership with enterprise email provider)
- **Integration:** Native Gmail/Outlook integration
- **Privacy-First:** On-device processing, enterprise-grade security

**How We'll Get There:**
- **Phase 1 (Year 1):** Augmentation (summarize, suggest replies)
- **Phase 2 (Year 2):** Automation (auto-respond to routine emails)
- **Phase 3 (Year 3):** Agentic (proactive email management)

**What We're NOT Doing:**
- NOT building another email client
- NOT targeting consumers (B2B only)
- NOT expanding to messaging/slack (email-only focus)

**How We'll Measure Success:**
- North Star: Hours saved per user per week
- Supporting: Email volume handled by AI, User satisfaction with AI responses

## üéØ Interview Preparation

### **Strategy Interview Questions**

**Q: "Walk me through how you develop product strategy."**

**Great Answer:**

"I use a structured approach:

**1. Market Understanding:** Deep dive into users, competitive landscape, trends. What's changing? Where are opportunities?

**2. Define Winning:** What does success look like? Vision ‚Üí Strategy ‚Üí Roadmap hierarchy.

**3. Make Choices:** Use frameworks like Playing to Win (Where to play? How to win?) or DHM (Delight in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways).

**4. Identify Moats:** What's our defensible advantage? Why can't competitors copy us?

**5. Validate:** Test strategy with customers, data, small experiments.

For example, [share specific strategy you developed and its impact]."

---

**Q: "How do you differentiate your product in a crowded market?"**

**Great Answer:**

"Differentiation starts with strategy, not features. I'd ask:

**1. Where can we win?** Not 'can we compete' but 'where do we have unique advantage?'

**2. Build moats:** What's hard to copy? Proprietary data? Network effects? Integration depth?

**3. Niche down:** Sometimes the best differentiation is specificity. 'AI writing tool for B2B SaaS marketers' beats 'AI writing tool.'

**4. Validate with users:** Does our differentiation matter to them?

For example, GitHub Copilot differentiated by exclusive GitHub data, deep IDE integration, and developer trust - hard for competitors to replicate."

## üé¨ Conclusion

Product strategy is the difference between building a product and building a winning product.

**Remember:**
- Strategy is about **choices** (what to do AND what not to do)
- Strategy is about **differentiation** (why you vs. competitors)
