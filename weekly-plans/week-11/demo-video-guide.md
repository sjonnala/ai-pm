## Part 1: Video Structure & Flow

### 1.1 Recommended Structure (8-10 minutes)

**Section 1: Introduction (1 minute)**
- Who you are
- What you built
- Why you built it
- What you'll show in this video

**Section 2: Problem Deep Dive (1.5 minutes)**
- User pain point (specific, relatable)
- Current solutions and their shortcomings
- Opportunity size
- Why this matters

**Section 3: Solution Overview (1 minute)**
- High-level: What is your product?
- How does it work? (1-2 sentences)
- Key innovation: Why AI-native?
- Main benefits

**Section 4: Live Product Demo (4 minutes) ‚≠ê CORE**
- **Scenario 1: Happy Path** (1.5 min)
  - Show primary use case working perfectly
  - Highlight AI capabilities
  - Emphasize speed/quality/value
- **Scenario 2: Advanced Feature** (1 min)
  - Demonstrate depth or sophisticated capability
  - Show how AI handles complexity
- **Scenario 3: Error Handling** (1 min)
  - Show graceful failure or edge case
  - Demonstrate UX thoughtfulness
  - Highlight guardrails
- **UX Highlights** (0.5 min)
  - Quick tour of key UX patterns
  - AI-native design choices

**Section 5: Technical Approach (1 minute)**
- Architecture overview (high-level diagram)
- AI/ML approach (what model/technique, why)
- Key technical decisions
- Trade-offs you considered

**Section 6: Impact & Learnings (1.5 minutes)**
- Success metrics (hypothetical or beta results)
- User feedback highlights
- Key learnings as a PM
- What you'd do differently
- Next steps / future roadmap

**Section 7: Closing (0.5 minutes)**
- Recap value proposition
- Thank viewer
- Share links (portfolio, LinkedIn, etc.)

## Part 5: Recording Best Practices

### 5.1 Recording Strategy

**Option 1: One-Take Wonder**
- Record the entire video start to finish
- **Pros:** Feels more natural, flows better
- **Cons:** Mistakes require full re-record

**Option 2: Section by Section**
- Record each section separately
- **Pros:** Easier to fix mistakes, can take breaks
- **Cons:** More editing, might feel disjointed

**Option 3: Hybrid**
- Record in 2-3 chunks (intro/demo/conclusion)
- **Pros:** Balance of flow and flexibility
- **Cons:** Some editing required

**Recommended:** Start with Option 2 for your first video. Once comfortable, try Option 1.

---

### 5.2 Speaking Tips

**Voice:**
- Speak slightly slower than normal conversation
- Vary your tone (don't be monotone)
- Project energy and enthusiasm (you're excited about this!)
- Smile when you talk (it changes your voice tone)
- Avoid filler words (um, uh, like, you know)

**Pacing:**
- Pause between sections (easier to edit)
- Don't rush through demos (viewers need time to absorb)
- Slow down for key points

**Delivery:**
- Sound confident (even if you're nervous)
- Be conversational (not reading a boring script)
- Imagine talking to a smart friend, not giving a presentation

---

### 5.3 Demo Recording Tips

**Before Each Demo:**
- State what you're about to show: "Let me show you how this works for a software engineer..."
- Ensure demo is ready (right screen, right state)

**During Demo:**
- **Move cursor intentionally:** Don't wave it around randomly
- **Highlight key UI elements:** Circle or point to important parts
- **Narrate your actions:** "I'm clicking on 'Generate Documentation'..."
- **Pause after actions:** Give viewers time to see results
- **Call out what's happening:** "Notice how the AI is analyzing the code structure..."

**If Something Goes Wrong:**
- Don't panic
- Pause recording (if recording sections)
- Fix the issue
- Resume or re-record that section
- **OR** keep going and edit it out later

**Pro Tip:** Record demos separately so you can re-do them without re-recording narration

---

### 5.4 Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Impact | Solution |
|---------|--------|----------|
| **Too long** | Viewers drop off | Stick to 8-10 min max, edit ruthlessly |
| **Poor audio** | Unwatchable | Invest in decent mic, quiet room |
| **Too fast** | Viewers confused | Slow down, especially during demos |
| **Too much jargon** | Alienates non-technical viewers | Explain terms, assume less knowledge |
| **No structure** | Viewers get lost | Follow clear structure (problem ‚Üí solution ‚Üí demo) |
| **Boring delivery** | Viewers tune out | Show enthusiasm, energy |
| **Doesn't work** | Embarrassing | Test everything beforehand |
| **No call-to-action** | No follow-up | Tell viewers where to learn more |

### 6.4 Audio Post-Production

**Basic Audio Improvements:**

- **Normalize volume:** Ensure consistent volume throughout
- **Remove background noise:** Use noise reduction filter
- **EQ (equalization):** Boost clarity, reduce muddiness
- **Compression:** Even out volume spikes

**Tools:**
- **Audacity:** Free audio editor
- **Adobe Audition:** Professional
- **Built-in tools:** Many video editors have audio tools

**When to Add Music:**

- **Background music:** Very subtle, during intro/outro only
- **Volume:** -20dB to -30dB (very quiet, just ambiance)
- **Avoid:** During demos or talking (too distracting)

### 7.2 Hosting Options

**Option 1: YouTube**
- **Pros:** Easy to share, embeddable, searchable, free, professional
- **Cons:** Public (unless unlisted), requires Google account
- **Privacy:** Set to "Unlisted" (only people with link can view)
- **Best for:** Portfolio sharing, wide distribution

**Option 2: Loom**
- **Pros:** Quick, easy, built-in viewer, engagement analytics
- **Cons:** Free tier limits (5 min), less professional than YouTube
- **Best for:** Quick shares, internal feedback

**Option 3: Vimeo**
- **Pros:** Professional, clean player, privacy controls
- **Cons:** Free tier limits, less discoverable than YouTube
- **Best for:** Portfolio, professional presentation

**Option 4: Google Drive**
- **Pros:** Easy sharing, no length limits, private
- **Cons:** Less professional, playback issues for some users
- **Best for:** Backup, sharing with specific people

**Recommendation:** Upload to YouTube (unlisted) + embed in portfolio site/Notion page

```
Software engineers spend an average of 3-5 hours per week writing and maintaining documentation. That's 15-25% of their time‚Äîtime that could be spent building features or fixing bugs.

Current solutions fall into two buckets:

First, manual documentation tools like Confluence or Notion. They're flexible, but they require engineers to manually write everything, keep it updated, and format it correctly. The result? Documentation becomes outdated almost immediately.

Second, auto-generated docs like Javadoc or Doxygen. These extract comments from code, but they're purely descriptive‚Äîthey don't explain the "why," the architecture, or how components fit together.

This isn't just frustrating for engineers. It costs businesses real money. Poor documentation leads to:
- Slower onboarding (new engineers take 2-3 months to get up to speed)
- More bugs (developers don't understand existing systems)
- Lost productivity (engineers waste time searching for information)

With over 20 million software engineers globally, and documentation being a universal pain point, there's a massive opportunity to use AI to solve this problem.

The question is: can AI understand code well enough to generate truly helpful documentation‚Äînot just descriptions, but explanations of how and why things work?
```
